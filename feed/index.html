WAREWOOF http://www.warewoof.com Mobile Design and Development Wed, 17 Jul 2013 04:25:53 +0000 en-US hourly 1 http://wordpress.org/?v=3.5.2 Why Automotive Apps are Still Years Away http://www.warewoof.com/why-automotive-apps-are-still-years-away/ http://www.warewoof.com/why-automotive-apps-are-still-years-away/#comments Thu, 04 Jul 2013 05:10:22 +0000 frank http://www.warewoof.com/?p=762 Mind-share for building automotive apps have steadily been growing over the last few years to warrant a recent article in The NYT.  Two primary catalysts have started this trend.  First being the technology/capability with the launch of iOS and Android and app economy, and it’s major appeal to consumers.  Second the ’07 economic downturn and rising gas prices since before that hit auto makers particularly hard in the wallets, they’re looking for high margin areas to grow their business.  Both forces are causing convergence of (personal/web) information and (in-vehicle) entertainment, or infotainment if you will.

So what we have essentially is an attempt to rethink or even Jerry-rig the consumer and mobile usage cases to translate into an in car environment, or even a BYOD type of setup.

The three largest issues standing in it’s way:

1. Pace of automotive product development due to auto grade requirements and reliability testing
New cars being sold today are still carrying middle-console technology that are at best 3 years old.  This is due to: 1) chip and component suppliers needing to auto-qualify their parts (~6-12 months), 2) automotive manufacturers to perform their own reliability testing beyond what is typical of consumer devices (~12 months), and 3) the unavoidable development time of said infotainment devices (~12 months at best).

2. Human usage factors
The industry is still in it’s infancy of this technology use thus mistakes are common and natural, and often breaking of the rule ‘don’t fix what’s not broken’.  For instance in the first iteration, the infotainment device was a purely touch screen device.  Using this would be akin to texting while driving.  They were missing physical cues and knobs, design choices that seem so obvious only in hindsight.  While each mistake should get fixed in the next iteration, drivers will have to wait the above mentioned 3 years.

3. Lack of popular standards
There are probably at least have a dozen proposed standards that propose to allow consumer devices to link and display on the in-vehicle display (allowing drivers to access their smartphone data/music playlists/phone contacts).  Which is fewer still than the number of app ecosystems each auto manufacturer is proposing to create for their drivers.  Developers and drivers will be rewarded or punished for whichever standards/ecosystems they decide to go with.

Ultimately, each industry goes through their own pace of development.  The auto industry is slow and aviation is much slower, both rightly so for sake of human safety.  In order to push the pace of innovation for non-critical systems however, producers should focus on the interfaces (human, electrical, and mechanical) and hardware requirements (temperature range, auto qualified, vibration, etc).  Publish defined interfaces and requirements and let the consumer device makers have at it.

Here’s a clean calculator site made by Google to walk you through the click counts and purchase percentages, from online views to offline sales, and come up with a dollar figure of what your mobile strategy is worth.

The overall formula is a summation of 4 separate components:

  1. In store sales started from mobile search
  2. Phone sales started from mobile search
  3. Mobile app download value started from search
  4. Cross-device (desktop e-commerce) sales started from mobile search

The each of the components are basically click-through percentages multiplied by average dollar amounts, for example:

No. of mobile searches and clicks

X  Store visit % (or phone call %, app download rate, desktop e-commerce sales, etc)

X  Avg. purchase $ (or phone purchase $, app lifetime value, online sales, etc)

=  component value


You can do this on the back of a napkin, or use a fancy walk-through calculator like this site (which might give you some additional insights since they break down several other metrics as well).  In either case, this is part of the due diligence businesses should perform when deciding their mobile strategy, and potentially lost opportunity cost for not taking action.

http://www.howtogomo.com/
